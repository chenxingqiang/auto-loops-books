Industrial inference fleets rarely standardize on a single accelerator class \citep{dao2023flashdecoding}. Hyperscale decode pools are still dominated by NVIDIA GPUs with Hopper- and Blackwell-generation tensor cores, asynchronous copy engines, and CUDA Graphs capture for launch reduction \citep{nvidia2024cudagraphs}. Cross-hardware compilation fails when teams optimize for peak FLOPs on one dimension while ignoring the others \citep{nvidia2022hopper}. \textbf{On-chip SRAM} caps how much of a decoder layer can remain resident between sub-operations; exceeding the cap forces spills to HBM or DDR, often erasing fusion wins \citep{amd2024aie}. \textbf{Parallel grain} determines whether a schedule is expressed as warps, OpenMP teams, or spatial lanes---mixing grains within one fused stage usually inserts barriers that decode cannot afford \citep{semianalysis2024tma}. \textbf{Dynamic shapes} separate server GPUs from edge NPUs: variable sequence lengths and batch sizes are routine in cloud decode, while many NPUs require compile-time specialization of buffer descriptors and DMA chains \citep{jeong2025activationswapping}.

Use the matrix as a checklist before writing a kernel or a compiler pass: if a proposed fusion increases SRAM footprint on GPU but removes a launch on CPU, the pass must expose both costs in the IR so autotuning can pick per target \citep{li2022compilerdrivensimu}. YiRage's chip-architecture modeling layer (Section~\ref{sec:yirage_modeling}) stores these dimensions as typed constraints rather than comments in CUDA source \citep{dlbook}.

NVIDIA decode optimization is bounded by the interaction of \textbf{warps}, \textbf{shared memory}, and \textbf{tensor cores}. Warps are the SIMT scheduling unit: branch divergence and barrier placement within a warp directly affect how much useful memory bandwidth is delivered per cycle. Shared memory is the programmer-visible on-chip scratchpad that makes attention and MLP fusion legal; its capacity (configuration-dependent on Hopper-class parts) is the hard ceiling for MegaKernel stages that refuse to spill to HBM. Tensor cores accelerate narrow GEMMs and attention inner loops, but only when layouts and instruction shapes match MMA requirements---decode's thin M matrices frequently under-fill tensor cores unless batched or fused with epilogue math.

Hopper-and-later generations add asynchronous copy (TMA) and cluster-scope synchronization, shifting the design question from ``how many kernels'' to ``how many bytes cross each hierarchy level per token''. CUDA Graphs reduce launch tax but do not relax bandwidth floors; they are complementary to fusion, not a substitute. Compiler passes that target GPUs must therefore reason about warp occupancy, shared-memory allocation, and tensor-core legality \emph{together}, not as independent knobs \citep{schmidt2024phdprojectcompiler}.

\section{CPU Constraints}
\label{sec:cpu_constraints}

CPU decode is often dismissed as slow, yet it remains operationally critical for hybrid serving, development parity, and regions without GPU quota. Performance is governed by \textbf{cache hierarchy}, \textbf{SIMD width}, and \textbf{NUMA} placement. Decoder layers stream large weight matrices through caches too small to retain full parameters; effective decode therefore depends on blocking GEMMs and attention so that hot tiles remain in L2/L3 across tokens. SIMD units (AVX-512, AMX, NEON) raise throughput only when layouts are contiguous and epilogues are vectorized---operator-at-a-time frameworks often leave SIMD under-used because each op reloads weights from memory.

\textbf{NUMA} effects appear when KV cache and weight shards land on remote sockets; a fusion pass that is neutral on a single-socket GPU build can regress on dual-socket Xeon hosts if buffer lifetimes ignore socket affinity. Hardware-aware CPU lowering must insert explicit tiling and layout transforms (NCHW vs.\ blocked layouts) and annotate parallel regions with cache-blocking sizes derived from L2/L3 measurements, not datasheet peaks alone.

\section{NPU Constraints}
\label{sec:npu_constraints}

Edge and embedded \textbf{NPU} accelerators optimize for energy per token and deterministic latency under watt caps. Many arrays---including AMD AI Engine tiles in XDNA products---implement \textbf{static} dataflow graphs with local SRAM per processing element and restricted dynamic reconfiguration \citep{amd2024xdna}. \textbf{Static} schedules mean buffer lifetimes, DMA descriptors, and fusion boundaries must be resolved at compile time; runtime op dispatch analogous to PyTorch eager mode is either unsupported or prohibitively expensive.

NPUs therefore amplify the Chapter~2 dataflow mindset: decode either fits as a spatial pipeline with on-chip residency, or it falls off the device to DDR and fails power envelopes. Dynamic shapes (variable context length, speculative decoding branches) require explicit recompilation or padded specialization; compiler stacks must surface these legality checks early rather than failing in backend codegen \citep{brauckmann2025dfanetacompilerspe}.

\section{Hardware Aware Compile}
\label{sec:hardware_aware_compile}

Hardware-aware compilation is the process of lowering the same high-level decoder IR while respecting per-target \textbf{tiling}, \textbf{layout}, and \textbf{pass} ordering constraints \citep{kumar2026hardwareawareneura}. \textbf{Tiling} chooses tile sizes for matmul and attention so that working sets fit GPU shared memory, CPU cache levels, or NPU PE buffers; illegal tiles are rejected before codegen, not discovered in profiling. \textbf{Layout} decisions (row-major vs.\ blocked layouts, swizzles for tensor cores, NPU DMA alignment) determine whether fused epilogues vectorize or silently scalarize. \textbf{Pass} pipelines order bufferization, fusion, layout normalization, and synchronization insertion differently per target: GPUs may fuse before aggressive layout transforms; NPUs may require lifetime analysis before any algebraic fusion.

A practical rule used throughout this book: every pass must declare which constraint dimension it optimizes (SRAM footprint, launch count, bandwidth, legality) and which hardware classes it applies to. Passes that only improve GPU tensor-core utilization but break NPU static schedules should be guarded by target predicates in the IR, not hidden in backend-specific forks. YiRage encodes these predicates in its chip-model interface so the same pass registry can run against Hopper, Zen, and XDNA profiles (Section~\ref{sec:yirage_modeling}).

Cross-hardware claims in this book use a single \textbf{benchmark} protocol so GPU ms/token numbers are comparable to CPU and NPU watts/token within the limits of each platform's transparency \citep{moon2025hybegpunpuhybridsy}. \textbf{Methodology} steps: fix model revision, tokenizer, context length, and batch size; warm caches and graphs; measure steady-state decode over at least 100 tokens; report median and p99 latency plus derived bytes/token and launches/token where host instrumentation is available. \section{YiRage Modeling}
\label{sec:yirage_modeling}

\textbf{YiRage} (YiRage) treats each deployment target as a typed \textbf{chip} \textbf{model}: a structured record of SRAM capacities, parallelism grains, DMA capabilities, and legality rules for dynamic shapes. Compiler passes query the model before fusion and tiling: a proposed MegaKernel stage must fit shared-memory and register budgets on Hopper, cache tiles on Zen, and PE-local buffers on XDNA \citep{kwon2023vllm}. The \textbf{model} is versioned per SKU so fleet operators can pin schedules to stepping-specific limits without forking source kernels.

Model-driven compilation does not replace measurement---it constrains search. Autotuning explores tile sizes and pipeline depths only within legally modeled envelopes, reducing wasted trials on configs that spill by construction. Later chapters show how the same interface backs CUDA TMA double-buffer pipelines (Chapter~8), XDNA buffer descriptors (Chapter~5), and cross-compiler benchmarks (Chapter~21).

\subsection{Worked Example: Layer Footprint on Hopper}
\label{sec:ch03_worked_hopper}

Consider a single decoder layer with hidden size 4096, batch-1, and FP16 weights. A naive operator schedule reads Q/K/V projection weights (${\sim}$48\,MiB combined), attention intermediates, and MLP matrices (${\sim}$96\,MiB) from HBM each token. Shared memory on a Hopper SM cannot hold the full footprint; fusion therefore targets \emph{slices}: a QKV stage that retains activations in shared memory while streaming one weight tile at a time through TMA. If the fused stage exceeds available shared memory after padding and barrier reservations, the compiler must split the stage---reintroducing launches---or accept HBM spill. This arithmetic is the GPU-side realization of Chapter~2's iron rules: registers and shared memory are contracts, not hints.

\subsection{CPU Roofline Sanity Check}
\label{sec:ch03_cpu_roofline}

On a dual-socket server CPU, decode GEMMs are often memory-bound at batch-1 even when L3 bandwidth looks ample on paper \citep{memoryfloor2026}. A roofline sanity check before enabling a CPU backend pass: estimate bytes moved per token (weights + KV + activations) and divide by measured L3/L2 throughput from \texttt{likwid} or vendor tools. If arithmetic intensity falls left of the roofline knee, SIMD widening alone cannot fix latency---tiling must increase reuse across tokens or fuse epilogues to cut reloads. NUMA-aware allocation matters when tensor parallel shards place half the weights on each socket: a ``fast'' kernel on socket 0 becomes slow if KV cache pages migrate to socket 1 mid-session.

\subsection{NPU Legality Gates}
\label{sec:ch03_npu_gates}

NPU compilation pipelines should expose explicit \textbf{legality gates} before codegen:
(1)~buffer descriptor alignment and maximum burst length,
(2)~static loop bounds for spatial arrays,
(3)~maximum on-chip buffer lifetime overlap,
(4)~supported element types for matrix units. Failing any gate must surface as a compile-time diagnostic with suggested padding or specialization strategies---not as a silent DDR spill at runtime. Edge products often ship with fixed model zoos; industrial compilers still need these gates for OEM variants that change SRAM sizes without changing the marketing SKU name.

\subsection{Anti-Patterns in Cross-Hardware Ports}
\label{sec:ch03_anti_patterns}

Three recurring anti-patterns destroy cross-hardware compiler credibility:
\textbf{(1)}~porting CUDA tile sizes directly to NPU PE arrays without remeasuring on-chip SRAM;
\textbf{(2)}~assuming CPU decode can reuse GPU fusion boundaries when weight layouts differ;
\textbf{(3)}~benchmarking only ms/token on GPUs while ignoring launches/token and bytes/token on all targets. Each anti-pattern is a residency failure expressed as a methodology failure---the fix is always to re-derive the dataflow contract for the target class before tuning instructions.

\subsection{Fleet Operations: Versioning Chip Models}
\label{sec:ch03_fleet_ops}

Production fleets mix GPU steppings, CPU generations, and NPU firmware revisions. YiRage-style \textbf{chip models} should be versioned in the same artifact registry as model weights: a decode graph compiled against Hopper-sm90 must not silently run on sm80 parts with narrower shared memory. Continuous integration should compile golden decoder snippets against every supported model profile and fail when legality checks regress. This operational discipline is what separates demo kernels from industrial inference platforms that survive silicon refresh cycles without rewriting megakernels by hand.

\subsection{Memory Hierarchy as a Compiler IR Problem}
\label{sec:ch03_memory_ir}

Treat memory hierarchy levels as \emph{address spaces} in compiler IR, not as comments in CUDA source. Global (HBM/DDR), shared (GPU) or L2/L3 hot sets (CPU), register files, and NPU tile memories each have distinct capacity, latency, and alignment rules. A hardware-aware lowering pass annotates each tensor value with a \texttt{memory.space} attribute and a proof obligation: ``this value remains in space S for N consumer ops without spill''. When the proof fails, the pass must either split fusion, change tiling, or reject the schedule---never silently rely on ``the runtime allocator will fix it''. This is how Chapter~2's residency philosophy becomes machine-checkable before any PTX or AIE graph is emitted.

GPU global memory is high bandwidth but high latency; shared memory is low latency but tiny; registers are fastest but drive occupancy cliffs. CPU caches are implicit---programmers do not name L2 explicitly---so CPU-oriented passes must infer residency from loop structure and tile sizes. NPUs expose intermediate buffers as explicit FIFOs between PE columns; residency is a schedule, not a cache. Cross-hardware stacks that unify these spaces behind one abstraction still need target-specific legality rules; pretending all memories are ``just buffers'' recreates the operator-first trap at IR level.

\subsection{Bandwidth Floors and Decode}
\label{sec:ch03_bandwidth_floors}

Decode at batch-1 repeatedly revisits the same weight tensors and growing KV cache for every output token. The \textbf{bandwidth} floor is therefore set by total bytes moved per token divided by achievable sustained HBM or DDR throughput---not by peak TFLOPs marketed on slide decks. Independent limit studies show that many decoder configurations are memory-bound across GPU generations even when tensor cores sit idle. Compiler optimizations that only reshape compute (e.g., different MMA shapes) without reducing bytes moved rarely move p99 latency once orchestration costs are counted.

Quantifying bytes/token requires consistent accounting: include KV read/write traffic, weight reads, activation spill traffic, and collective shards in tensor-parallel deployments. Large MoE models add expert routing metadata and conditional weight reads; the constraint matrix must extend with ``conditional bandwidth'' rows in those chapters. Hardware-aware compilers should emit byte estimates alongside schedules so fleet planners can compare silicon options before buying accelerators \citep{bernardo2025compilerawareaihar}.

\subsection{Parallelism Grain and Occupancy}
\label{sec:ch03_parallel_grain}

\textbf{Parallelism} grain determines how work maps to hardware threads. GPUs use warps of 32 lanes; CPUs use cores and SIMD lanes; NPUs use spatially placed PEs with fixed throughput per cycle. Decode kernels with tiny per-token M/N dimensions often launch wide grids where most lanes idle---effective occupancy collapses even when ``GPU utilization'' dashboards show non-zero values. Fusion raises arithmetic intensity per launch but tightens shared-memory occupancy; the compiler must co-optimize grain and footprint.

On CPUs, grain choices interact with OpenMP scheduling and hyperthreads; on NPUs, grain is frequently fixed at compile time. A portable IR should represent parallel dimensions explicitly so backends can remap grains rather than baking warp assumptions into target-independent passes. YiRage's chip models include a \texttt{parallel.grain} field consumed by tiling heuristics in Chapters~6--9.

\subsection{Dynamic Shapes and Specialization}
\label{sec:ch03_dynamic_shapes}

Cloud decode workloads exhibit \textbf{dynamic shapes}: variable context length, variable batch from continuous batching, and speculative decoding branches. GPUs and CPUs tolerate dynamic shapes with runtime kernel selection, CUDA Graphs updates, or recompilation caches. Many NPUs require shape specialization or upper-bound padding encoded in buffer descriptors. Hardware-aware compilers therefore need two pipelines: a \emph{legalize dynamic shapes} pass for flexible targets and a \emph{specialize bounds} pass for static targets.

Specialization interacts with fleet operations: over-specializing to one context length improves peak performance but explodes compile-matrix size. Industrial systems cap specialization fanout and fall back to padded shapes with masked compute---the constraint matrix should record which targets support masking efficiently. Chapter~12 compares eager, graph, and MegaKernel execution modes through this specialization lens.

\subsection{Tensor Cores vs Memory: When MMA Helps Decode}
\label{sec:ch03_tensor_core_decode}

Tensor cores accelerate GEMM-like primitives but decode GEMMs are often ``thin''. Hardware-aware layout passes exist to reshape decode matmuls into MMA-friendly tiles; without them, frameworks may still call tensor-core libraries yet achieve CPU-like effective throughput because epilogues and reductions scalarize. The compiler must fuse epilogues (bias, activation, residual) into the same stage that issues MMA instructions; otherwise bandwidth dominates regardless of core type.

Hopper-and-later TMA paths move data asynchronously into shared memory while MMA units work on the previous tile---double-buffer pipelines in Chapter~8 depend on the constraints documented here. Blackwell-era evolutions continue widening MMA shapes and cluster scopes; chip models must version these fields so autotuning does not explore illegal instruction forms.

\subsection{Synchronization Cost in Constraint Planning}
\label{sec:ch03_sync_preview}

Every fusion decision trades bytes moved against \textbf{sync} introduced. GPU block barriers, CPU fences, and NPU cascade stalls have different magnitudes; a fusion legal on GPU may be illegal on NPU if it requires global barriers between PE columns. Hardware-aware passes should estimate sync overhead alongside memory traffic---Chapter~9 formalizes hierarchical sync design; this chapter records why sync belongs in the constraint matrix, not as an afterthought.

Launch orchestration on the host is itself a synchronization tax: thousands of kernels per token imply thousands of implicit host-device sync points unless graphs or megakernels collapse them. Constraint-aware compilation targets \emph{launch count} as a first-class metric equal to bytes/token.

\subsection{Reading Vendor Documentation Critically}
\label{sec:ch03_vendor_docs}

Silicon vendors document peaks: TFLOPs, TB/s, ``AI TOPS.'' Production decode rarely sits at peaks. Compiler engineers should translate datasheet peaks into sustained bounds using microbenchmarks on their fleet SKUs, then store results in chip models. Second-source validation (academic limit studies, independent roofline analyses) prevents single-vendor optimism from leaking into pass heuristics.

When documentation omits constraints (exact shared-memory banking rules, DMA alignment minima), derive them empirically and check them into versioned model files rather than scattering magic numbers through kernels. This discipline is especially important for NPUs where public documentation is thinner than CUDA's.

\subsection{Quantitative Constraint Checklist}
\label{sec:ch03_checklist}

Before merging any hardware-aware pass, answer the following quantitatively on \emph{each} target SKU:
\begin{enumerate}
\item What is the on-chip SRAM budget per fused stage (bytes) and does the schedule include barrier/shared-memory padding overhead ? \item What is estimated HBM/DDR bytes per output token after fusion, including KV traffic at the deployment context length ? \item How many host-visible launches remain per token after graph capture or MegaKernel lowering ? \item Does the schedule require dynamic shapes illegal on NPU, and if so, what specialization or padding strategy is selected ? \item What sync points exist between parallel grains, and can any be moved inside on-chip residency regions ? \end{enumerate}
Passes that cannot answer these five questions should remain behind feature flags until chip models supply defaults.

\subsection{Industrial Case Patterns}
\label{sec:ch03_case_patterns}

\textbf{Case A — GPU decode pool:} Operators see high HBM utilization but flat ms/token; constraint analysis shows launches/token in the thousands. Fix path: graph capture plus shared-memory fusion guided by chip model shared-memory caps. \textbf{Case B — Hybrid CPU+GPU:} CPU preprocessing becomes tail-latency outlier due to NUMA-remote KV pages. Fix path: NUMA-aware bufferization pass pinning KV shards. \textbf{Case C — Edge NPU:} Model fits in marketing TOPS claims but misses real-time factor at batch-1. Fix path: static MegaKernel with explicit DMA schedule and reduced DDR touches. These patterns recur in Part~VI case studies; documenting them here gives readers a failure-mode index before diving into compiler-specific chapters.

\subsection{Tooling Interfaces for Chip Models}
\label{sec:ch03_tooling}

Chip models should be consumable by three tools: autotuners, legality checkers, and benchmark harnesses. Autotuners read SRAM and register caps to bound tile search. Legality checkers validate DMA alignment and static-loop requirements before codegen. Benchmark harnesses attach bytes/token and launches/token calculators to every timed configuration. Human-readable YAML or JSON models checked into git beat ad-hoc spreadsheets because they version alongside compiler passes in CI. When a new GPU stepping ships, update the model file first, rerun golden tests second, and only then re-open autotuning campaigns \citep{malbaa2024modelinferenceusin}.

\subsection{Pedagogical Summary for Compiler Engineers}
\label{sec:ch03_pedagogy}

Students of AI compilers should memorize the constraint matrix before memorizing MMA instruction shapes \citep{ludmir2024parallaxacompilerf}. The exam question is not ``write a FlashAttention kernel'' but ``given a decoder layer and three silicon profiles, which fusions are legal and which metrics will move''. That question format mirrors production design reviews and prevents teams from shipping GPU-only wins that break NPU deployments. Carry this chapter's tables into every later pass review until hardware-aware compilation becomes reflex, not ceremony.

\subsection{Energy and Power as First-Class Constraints}
\label{sec:ch03_energy}

Edge \textbf{NPU} deployments add \textbf{energy} per token and thermal caps that GPUs in datacenters can ignore at batch-1. A schedule that minimizes HBM bytes often minimizes energy as well; schedules that thrash DDR due to spills can exceed TDP even when latency looks acceptable in lab tests. Hardware-aware compilers for edge should therefore log estimated off-chip bytes and DMA activations alongside latency. Datacenter GPUs are not immune---power caps throttle clock when sustained memory traffic keeps memory controllers busy.

\subsection{Security and Multi-Tenant Interactions}
\label{sec:ch03_security}

Multi-tenant GPU fleets introduce constraints unrelated to FLOPs: partitioned SM quotas, encrypted KV regions, and orchestration policies that forbid certain persistent kernels. A MegaKernel optimized for bare-metal may be illegal under MIG or certain confidential-computing modes. Hardware-aware passes should accept policy flags in chip models (e.g., ``graph capture allowed'', ``persistent kernels allowed'') so optimizations do not violate serving security contracts. These flags are operational realities in industrial inference, not edge cases \citep{surez2025energyefficientinf}.

Chapter~2 asked teams to draw dataflow before profiling kernels; this chapter asks them to attach numeric hardware bounds to every edge in that drawing. \subsection{Handoff to Chapter~4}
\label{sec:ch03_handoff}

Chapter~4 narrows the lens to NVIDIA Hopper and Blackwell: TMA, cluster shared memory, and tensor-core evolution. Readers should enter that chapter with a completed constraint matrix for at least one NVIDIA SKU and one non-GPU target. The Hopper deep dive is not a departure from cross-hardware thinking---it is the reference GPU implementation against which CPU and NPU schedules are compared in later parts. Keep bytes/token and launches/token on the same spreadsheet as you read forward; Chapters~4--5 add mechanism, not new success metrics.

Industrial teams sometimes treat hardware chapters as ``for kernel engineers only.''
That separation fails in practice: compiler passes encode the same SRAM and bandwidth limits documented here. When compiler and kernel teams share one chip model repository, regressions surface as legality check failures instead of week-long email threads about mystery slowdowns. Invest up front in shared constraint artifacts; it is cheaper than reconciling divergent assumptions after a silicon refresh.

\textbf{Closing metric reminder:} decode optimization is judged on sustained bytes/token, launches/token, and p99 latency under your real batching policy---not on kernel-local microbenchmarks. Hardware-aware compilation exists to keep those fleet metrics inside SLO as models, context lengths, and silicon generations change.

Fleet operators should snapshot constraint matrices per SKU quarterly: stepping changes, firmware updates, and driver releases can shift achievable sustained bandwidth without changing product names. Treat those snapshots as inputs to chip models, not as slide-deck trivia. When models and measurements disagree, trust measured sustained bytes/token on your binaries first, then reconcile the model---never the reverse.

This discipline is boring until a driver upgrade silently shifts shared-memory defaults; teams with versioned chip models catch the shift in CI, not in customer tickets.

Document every constraint assumption in git next to the passes that consume it; future you will not remember which workshop whiteboard justified a tile size.

Hardware constraints are the floor today; megakernels and compilers are how you build on it. Fleet-wide SLO reviews should cite this chapter's constraint matrix before approving any new fusion pass in production LLM serving fleets \citep{taxbreak2026}.
